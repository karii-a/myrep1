Information Systems Strategy

IS vs IT · Strategic Objectives · Worldwide Changes · IS Constraints

What is Strategy?

A strategy is a deliberate course of action and a specification of the resources required to achieve a specific objective. It sets the overall direction for an entire business — determining which markets to enter, how to deal with rivals, and what capabilities to build.

IS vs IT — The Critical Distinction

The Car and the Road Analogy: Information Systems (IS) = the car — the vehicle that creates value and defines WHAT the business needs. Information Technology (IT) = the road — the infrastructure that answers HOW to deliver it.
Information Systems (IS) — The WHAT

A collection of hardware, software, data, people, and processes that work together to produce quality information for decision-making. IS defines what the business team wants to achieve. It is business-focused — creating value, supporting processes, enabling decisions.

Information Technology (IT) — The HOW

The underlying technology — hardware, software, networks, operating systems, and data management — that makes IS possible. IT is the technical foundation: skills to deploy infrastructure and build apps. IT answers how to supply what the business team needs.

IS/IT Alignment

IS/IT Alignment refers to the degree to which an organization's IS/IT department understands the business goals and uses its resources to support those goals. Misalignment = wasted investment and missed opportunities.

Business Model

The method of doing business — who your customers are, how you capture profit, and what value you offer. Defines the "why" behind IS investment.

Business Strategy

Sets overall direction — which market to enter, how to deal with rivals. The big-picture plan IS must support.

IS Strategy (What)

What systems, information, and IT structure does the business need to succeed and gain competitive advantage?

IT Strategy (How)

How the organization will deliver and manage technology, infrastructure, and IT services to support IS and business strategies.

Six Strategic Business Objectives of IS

Organizations invest in IS to effectively support and enable the following strategic objectives:

1
Operational ExcellenceEfficiency improvement for higher profitability. Example: Amazon automates warehouses, tracks inventory in real time, and optimises delivery routes — resulting in faster delivery, lower costs, and higher efficiency.
2
New Products and ServicesEnabling innovation and market expansion. Example: Uber's entire service is built on IS (GPS, mobile apps, payment systems) — it created a new service model without owning a single car.
3
Customer and Supplier IntimacyServing customers well leads to returning revenue; supplier intimacy lowers costs. Example: Amazon uses customer data to recommend products based on past purchases — leading to better experience and repeat purchases.
4
Improved Decision MakingEliminating guesswork, overproduction, and resource misallocation. Example: stores use POS sales data to decide exactly how much stock to order — avoiding overstocking or running out of products.

6
SurvivalTechnology as a business necessity due to industry changes or regulations. Example: during COVID, restaurants that adopted online ordering and digital payments survived — those that didn't struggled to stay open.

IS Strategy Constraints

Lack of Awareness

Stakeholders don't understand the impact of IS on business outcomes — so they underinvest or misalign technology with goals.

Credibility Gap

Perceived gap in the reliability or actual value of IS — management doesn't trust that IS delivers what it promises.

Resource Mismanagement

Not treating information as a strategic business resource to be actively managed — leading to poor data quality and wasted systems.

Financial Myopia

Demanding immediate financial justification for every IS investment — blocking long-term infrastructure that pays off over years.

Short-Term Focus

Prioritising immediate gains over long-term strategic architecture — systems become outdated, fragmented, and impossible to integrate.

Porter's Five Forces Model

Competitive Analysis · Industry Profitability · Strategic Positioning

Developed in 1979 by Michael Porter at Harvard Business School, this model provides a comprehensive view of the competitive environment. It identifies the five fundamental pressures that shape the fate of a firm and determine an industry's long-term profitability potential. Before choosing how to compete, you must understand what you are competing against.

Key Insight: The goal is not just to analyse industry forces, but to find a strategic position that best defends against them — or better yet, shapes them in your favour. Strategy is about influencing the forces, not just reacting to them.

Force 1 — Competitive Rivalry

1
Competitive Rivalry

Describes the intensity of competition among existing players. High rivalry erodes profits through price wars, advertising battles, and innovation races. When rivalry is weak, companies can raise prices and earn more. This is the central force — the others all feed into it.

🔴 Threat to Profit (High Rivalry)
  • Numerous competitors
  • Slow industry sales growth
  • Low product differentiation
  • High exit barriers (e.g., specialised equipment)
  • Similar cost structures across firms
🟢 Opportunity to Profit (Low Rivalry)
  • Few competitors
  • Rapid industry sales growth
  • Significant product differentiation
  • Minimal exit barriers
  • Strong brand identities
📌 Case Study

In the mature smartphone market, Apple and Samsung are locked in constant feature releases and massive marketing spend — both eroding profit gains for both sides. High rivalry = pressure on margins.

Force 2 — Threat of New Entrants

2
Threat of New Entrants

Measures how easily new competitors can enter your industry and take market share. "Barriers to entry" are the obstacles that protect incumbents. High barriers = low threat = preserved profitability. Low barriers = constant flood of new challengers.

🔴 High Threat / Easy Entry
  • Weak product differentiation
  • Low brand loyalty among customers
  • Minimal switching costs
  • Low start-up capital requirements
  • Common, accessible technology
🟢 Low Threat / Difficult Entry
  • Strong brand preferences
  • High capital requirements
  • Patents or proprietary technology
  • Economies of scale advantages
  • Government regulation / licencing
📌 Case Study

Airlines vs. App Market: Airlines require billions in capital, FAA approvals, and airport slots — extremely high barriers. The app market needs only a laptop and App Store registration — constant flood of new challengers threatens every incumbent like Microsoft Office.

Force 3 — Bargaining Power of Buyers

3
Bargaining Power of Buyers

Buyers with strong bargaining power can force prices down, demand higher quality, or require more services — capturing value from suppliers and reducing industry profitability. When buyers are weak, suppliers set terms.

🔴 Powerful Buyers (Threat to Suppliers)
  • Purchases in large volumes
  • Products are standard / undifferentiated
  • Buyer has full price information
  • Low switching costs for the buyer
  • Buyer could produce the product itself
🟢 Weak Buyers (Opportunity for Suppliers)
  • Purchases in small volumes
  • Products are highly differentiated
  • Buyer has limited market information
  • High switching costs
  • Products are critical to buyer's operation
📌 Case Study

Walmart's immense scale means it can demand dramatically lower prices from suppliers. The retailer's purchase volume gives it enormous leverage in negotiations — suppliers either comply or lose the account entirely.

Force 4 — Bargaining Power of Suppliers

4
Bargaining Power of Suppliers

Powerful suppliers can raise input prices or reduce quality — driving up costs for producers and squeezing margins. Suppliers have the power to drive costs. This is the mirror of buyer power.

🔴 Powerful Suppliers (Threat to Buyers)
  • Limited number of suppliers exist
  • No real substitute for their product
  • Highly differentiated products
  • High switching costs
  • Supplier could become a competitor
🟢 Weak Suppliers (Opportunity for Buyers)
  • Many alternative suppliers available
  • Undifferentiated products (commodities)
  • Minimal switching costs
  • Buyer is a major customer for the supplier
  • Easy forward integration not possible
📌 Case Study

TSMC dominates manufacturing of advanced chips for Apple and Nvidia. With no viable alternative fabs and massive capital costs, TSMC has the leverage to influence prices and delivery timelines for even the largest tech companies.

Force 5 — Threat of Substitutes

5
Threat of Substitutes

Substitutes are products or services from a different industry that offer a similar benefit. They place a ceiling on the prices firms can charge — limiting profits. The threat is not from direct competitors but from alternatives that serve the same need.

🔴 High Threat of Substitution
  • Substitute offers better price-performance
  • Easy for customers to switch
  • Customers have little brand loyalty
  • Switching costs are low
  • Substitute improves rapidly
🟢 Low Threat of Substitution
  • No good substitutes available
  • Product offers unique benefit
  • High switching costs for customers
  • Strong emotional brand attachment
  • Regulatory or safety barriers
📌 Case Study

Netflix provided a powerful substitute for cable TV. The lower cost, better user experience, and on-demand access led to widespread cord-cutting — completely disrupting the cable industry within a decade.

Economies of Scale

A concept critical to understanding barriers to entry and cost leadership. Economies of scale is the reduction in per-unit cost of production when production volume increases. Fixed costs get spread over more units.

Worked Example

Fixed cost of $1,000 needed to produce 10 units of Pencil A → Cost = $100 per pencil.

Same $1,000 fixed cost to produce 50 units → Cost = $20 per pencil.

This means large incumbents can undercut new entrants on price while still making a profit — new entrants simply cannot match the per-unit cost of established players. This is why Economies of Scale are a key barrier to entry.

Applying the Framework — 4-Step Process

1
Define the IndustryClearly specify the industry you're analysing — its scope and boundaries. "Smartphones" vs "consumer electronics" will yield very different analyses.
2
Analyse the ForcesIdentify key players (competitors, customers, suppliers), potential substitutes, and entry barriers for the defined industry. Gather evidence for each force.
3
Assess AttractivenessFor each force, determine if factors make the industry easy or difficult. Look at the total picture to conclude about structural profitability — is this industry worth competing in?
4
Formulate StrategyUse your analysis to identify opportunities to reduce competitive pressures and find a strategic position where the forces are weakest — then design your competitive strategy accordingly.

Competitive Strategies

Porter's Generic Strategies · Six Extended Strategies · SWOT · First Mover

Once you understand the forces shaping your industry, you face the most critical question: How will you compete? Success isn't a random accident — it stems from a clear, conscious choice about where to play and how to win.

Porter's Generic Strategies — The 2×2 Matrix

Porter's framework asks two questions: Will you win on cost or differentiation? Will you target a mass market or a niche? The intersection of these answers gives you four generic strategies.

🟠 LOW COST
🟢 DIFFERENTIATION
MASS MARKET
Cost Leadership

Serve broad market with lowest production cost. Win by being the undisputed cheapest producer. E.g. Walmart, Xiaomi

Differentiation Leadership

Offer unique features the broad market values enough to pay a premium. E.g. Apple, Tesla

NICHE MARKET
Cost Focus

Be cheapest producer within a specific niche. E.g. corporate bulk tote bags, budget airlines on specific routes

Differentiation Focus

Uniquely premium within a specific niche. E.g. Rolls Royce, Titleist golf bags, Peloton

Strategy 1 — Cost Leadership

Win by Being the Undisputed Low-Cost Producer

Serve a broad market with the lowest production cost in the industry. The goal is not to be cheap — it is to achieve the lowest cost structure relative to rivals, which allows you to either undercut on price or earn higher margins at the same price. Achieved through economies of scale, process efficiency, technology investment, and tight supply chain control.

WalmartXiaomiRyanairAmazon (logistics)

Strategy 2 — Differentiation

Win by Being Uniquely Valuable to Your Customer

Offer unique features that a broad market values and is willing to pay a premium price for. Differentiation creates customer loyalty and isolates the firm from competitive rivalry — if customers believe no other product is quite like yours, they'll pay more and switch less. The key is that uniqueness must be perceived and valued by the customer — not just technically superior.

AppleTeslaDysonStarbucks

Strategy 3 — Focus / Niche

Win by Being the Unique Expert for a Niche

Serve a narrow market segment with a highly tailored offering. Can be either cost focus (cheapest within niche) or differentiation focus (most premium within niche). The idea is that by focusing intensely, you understand your niche customers better than any broad competitor can — and therefore serve them more effectively.

Rolls Royce (ultra-luxury niche) · IKEA corporate bulk (cost niche) · Titleist (golf equipment niche)

The Extended Strategies (Beyond Porter)

4
Innovation — Win by Being InnovativeContinuously develop new products, processes, or business models to stay ahead. Companies invest in R&D to create breakthrough products or models that disrupt competitors and create entirely new demand. Netflix: shifted from DVD rental → streaming → original content production, rethinking the entire model at each stage.
5
Growth — Win by Being Relentless in ExpansionExpand market share through organic growth (new markets, new products) or inorganic methods (acquisitions, mergers). Growth expands scale, boosting revenues and economies of scale. Amazon: books → e-commerce → AWS cloud → logistics → healthcare. Nepal's Chaudhary Group: Wai Wai noodles → banking → telecom (CG Digital) → retail.
6
Alliance — Win by Being Powerful Through Strategic PartnershipForm strategic partnerships, joint ventures, or networks to access resources, markets, or capabilities without the full cost of ownership. Alliances pool resources via partnerships to access new capabilities, markets, or spread risk. Samsung + Google: share platforms and innovation to strengthen the Android ecosystem without merging. OpenAI + Microsoft: access advanced capabilities through alliance rather than internal development.

SWOT Analysis

SWOT analysis is a strategic planning framework that evaluates an organisation's internal strengths and weaknesses alongside external opportunities and threats. Businesses use it to inform decision-making and strategy development.

S — Strengths (Internal)
  • What does the company do well?
  • Is the company strong in its market?
  • Does the company have a strong culture?
  • Unique resources, patents, brand loyalty
  • Cost advantages, superior technology
W — Weaknesses (Internal)
  • What does the company do poorly?
  • What problems could be avoided?
  • Does the company have serious financial liabilities?
  • Lack of skills, limited capacity
  • Poor IS integration, outdated systems
O — Opportunities (External)
  • Are industry trends moving upward?
  • Do new markets exist for products/services?
  • Are there new technologies to exploit?
  • Competitor weaknesses to exploit
  • Government incentives, policy changes
T — Threats (External)
  • What are competitors doing well?
  • What obstacles does the company face?
  • Troubling changes in regulations or technology?
  • New entrants disrupting the market
  • Supply chain disruptions, economic shifts
Nepal Startup Example — "NepalChain Hub"

Strength User-friendly app with real-time GPS tracking. Strong local partnerships with firms like DHL Nepal.

Weakness Dependence on inconsistent internet in rural areas. Small dev team limits AI forecasting features.

Opportunity Boom in e-commerce post-2025 (Daraz, Sastodeal). Government incentives for IT startups in Bagmati Province.

Threat Competition from global giants entering South Asia. Supply chain disruptions from monsoons and regional regulations.

First Mover Advantage

A first mover is an organisation that is the first to bring a new product innovation to the marketplace or to use a new process innovation. Being first allows it to capture market share, build brand loyalty, and establish barriers before rivals arrive.

Advantages Disadvantages
Reputation for being innovative — brand becomes synonymous with the category High uncertainty over exact direction technology will take
Cost leader benefits from moving along experience curve first Risk of competitors imitating innovations (free-rider effect — they copy at lower cost)
Control over scarce assets (prime shelf space, key suppliers, patents) preventing others High financial and strategic risks — may invest heavily in the wrong direction
Opportunity to build customer relationships and loyalty before rivals exist High development costs — later entrants learn from your mistakes for free
Classic Example: Coca-Cola entered the cola market first in 1886. More than a century later, it still dominates — its brand loyalty and global distribution network remain nearly impossible to replicate.

Supply Chain Management I

What is a Supply Chain · SCM Objectives · Uncertainty · Enablers · ESG

What is a Supply Chain?

A Supply Chain (also called a supply network or supply web) is the entire network of individuals, organisations, resources, activities, and technology involved in the creation and sale of a product. It spans everything from the delivery of source materials from the supplier to the manufacturer, through to its eventual delivery to the end user.

Simple Examples:
Fridge: Raw materials → manufacturers → transporters → retailers → customer.
Jeans: Cotton farms → textile mills → garment factories → distributors → retail stores.
Dell: sources components from Intel, assembles PCs, delivers directly to customers via UPS.

Supply Chain Tiers

Supply chains are structured in tiers — each tier is further from the end customer:

Tier 1 (Closest to Customer)

Direct suppliers and retailers who interact most closely with the end consumer. In denim jeans: the garment assembly factory and the retail store.

Tier 2 (Components)

Suppliers to Tier 1 — they make components used in assembly. In denim jeans: the textile mill that makes the denim fabric from cotton.

Tier 3 (Raw Materials)

The furthest upstream — source of raw materials. In denim jeans: cotton farms in the US and India, indigo dye producers in China.

Supply Chain Activities

Primary Activities (Value-Creating)

1. Inbound Logistics — receiving and storing raw materials
2. Operations — manufacturing, testing, assembly
3. Outbound Logistics — storage and distribution
4. Marketing and Sales — branding, pricing, promotion
5. Service — after-sales support, returns

Support Activities (Enabling)

1. Firm Infrastructure — accounting and finance
2. Human Resources — hiring, training, development
3. Technology Development — use of IT/IS systems
4. Procurement — negotiation, supplier selection, buying

Supply Chain Management — Core Definition

SCM is about making sure information moves so well that the company can give customers exactly what they want, exactly when they want it, without wasting money on excess inventory. It coordinates the flow of products, information, and finances across the entire chain.

Four Keys to Effective SCM

1. Information

What and how much to produce. Need data regarding inventory levels, customer orders, delivery status. Without accurate information, companies over-produce or under-produce — both are expensive.

2. Cooperation

Working together toward a common goal — aligning production schedules, helping suppliers improve their processes, and sharing risk. A chain is only as strong as its weakest link.

3. Communication

Exchange of information between all parties — suppliers, logistics, and retailers. The right information must reach the right party at the right time to prevent costly disruptions.

4. Trust

All parties must act in good faith, keep their word, and share data without exploiting each other. Without trust, every transaction requires verification — dramatically increasing cost and slowing response.

Supply Chain Objectives

①
Customer Satisfaction FirstThe customer is the ONLY source of actual cash entering the supply chain. Every payment between SC partners is simply redistribution of that same money. Satisfying the end customer is the only way new cash enters the system.
②
Maximize Overall Value GeneratedSupply Chain Value = What the customer pays − Total costs across ALL stages. A chain generating high value is an efficient chain. High value means maximum surplus is created and retained across the system.
③
Share Profitability Across PartnersProfitability should be shared among all partners — suppliers, shippers, retailers — so the whole system stays healthy and motivated. A chain where only the retailer profits will eventually lose its suppliers.
④
Measure Total SC Profitability — Not One StageNever judge a supply chain by one stage's success. If the shipping department saves money using a slow boat, but the retail store loses customers because the product is out of stock — the TOTAL supply chain has failed, even if shipping looks great on its own.

Supply Chain Uncertainty Factors

1. Inaccurate Demand Forecasting

Most common issue. If you forecast 100 units but customers buy 10 — you're stuck with waste. If they buy 200 — you run out. Both are costly in different ways.

2. Long / Variable Lead Times

The gap between ordering a part and receiving it. If a supplier says "5 to 10 days," that 5-day window makes perfect production scheduling impossible — you must pad everything with buffer time.

3. Late Deliveries & Incomplete Shipments

Even short lead times mean nothing if deliveries are unreliable. A truck arriving late or with half the order can stop an entire assembly line.

4. Price Fluctuations

Sudden changes in raw material costs (fuel, steel, cotton) make budgeting impossible and force reactive pricing decisions that damage customer trust.

Supply Chain Uncertainty — Impacts

Lateness and Incomplete Orders

End customers get frustrated when they don't receive what they ordered on time. This directly damages brand reputation and drives customers to competitors — a chain reaction that costs far more than the missed delivery itself.

Safety Stock (Inventory Insurance)

Companies keep "extra" inventory just in case — called Safety Stock. While it prevents stockouts, it is very expensive: it ties up cash, requires warehouse space, and risks obsolescence if demand changes.

SC Enablers — Technologies That Make SCM Efficient

Technology What It Does Example

EDI (Electronic Data Interchange) Moving away from paper-based documents to standardised electronic documents — orders, invoices, shipping notices. Walmart requiring EDI from all suppliers

Benefits of Integrating IS/IT into SCM

Cost Savings

Reduced operational costs through automation, elimination of intermediaries, and optimised inventory levels across the chain.

Faster Response

Shortening SC response and transaction times — orders processed in seconds instead of days, enabling same-day fulfillment.

Global Access

Gaining access to global markets, suppliers, and distribution channels that would be impossible without digital systems.

Better Data

Collection and analysis of vast amounts of customer data and preferences — enabling personalised offers and smarter inventory decisions.

Fraud Protection

Electronic payments are easier to audit and monitor — reducing theft, fraud, and error compared to paper-based systems.

Competitive Bidding

Easier to obtain competitive bid information — simpler to negotiate price and delivery terms with multiple suppliers simultaneously.

Duties, Tariffs & Trade

Duty vs Tariff

Duty = the actual tax paid on goods as they enter a country.
Tariff / Customs Duty = the rate or schedule that tells how much duty to charge.
Trade Agreements = groups of nations agree to uniform tariffs, creating internal competitive advantages between member nations. Examples: EU, ASEAN, NAFTA.

Global Trade Specialists

Professionals who support international trade operations:
• Freight Forwarders — arrange physical transportation
• Customs House Brokers — handle import/export regulations
• Export Packers — specialist packaging for international shipping
• Export Management Companies — manage entire export process

ESG in Supply Chain Management

Environmental

Carbon footprint assessment — tracking greenhouse gas emissions across transportation, manufacturing, and energy usage. Resource use evaluation — water, energy, and raw materials. Sustainable packaging and reverse logistics for returns.

Social

Fair labour practices across the entire supply chain — not just within the company but verifying suppliers' working conditions. Community impact assessments for manufacturing locations.

Governance

Ethical business practices — ensuring ethical conduct and integrity in all aspects of SC including anti-corruption measures and compliance with laws and regulations. Transparent supplier auditing.

Managing Global Systems

Globalization · Org Structures · Global Strategies · BI · GIS Challenges

What is Globalization?

Globalization is a process by which businesses develop international influence or start operating on an international scale. Advances in transportation and telecommunications infrastructure — including the rise of the Internet — are major factors, generating further interdependence of economic and cultural activities.

Key Insight: Globalization isn't just a business choice — it's a result of cultural evolution. Because we talk, travel, and think more alike than ever before, businesses are practically forced to coordinate their operations on a global scale to remain competitive.

Factors Driving Internationalization

General Cultural Factors

• Global communication and transportation technologies
• Development of a global culture (shared tastes, media, brands)
• Emergence of global social norms
• Political stability in key regions
• Global knowledge base and education

Specific Business Factors

• Access to global markets and customers
• Global production and operations
• Global coordination and logistics
• Access to global workforce and talent
• Global economies of scale

Four Global Business Strategies

Domestic Exporter

Heavy centralisation — all corporate activities focused in the home country. Products are made at home and simply shipped to foreign markets with minimal local adaptation. Low risk, low global presence.

Multinational

Financial management and control are dispersed to local units to adapt to specific markets. Each country unit operates with significant autonomy — adapting products, marketing, and strategy to local conditions.

Franchiser

Business models created at home are copied exactly in foreign locations. The core product and processes are standardised globally, but delivery is local. Example: KFC, McDonald's — the menu and cooking process are the same, but local operators run the stores.

Transnational

All units participate as equals in a value-adding partnership. Knowledge, resources, and capabilities flow across the whole network. Example: Ford/GM — shared and standardised engines developed collaboratively across countries.

Four System Configurations (Technical Setup)

1
CentralizedDevelopment and operations occur totally at the domestic home base. Example: every branch globally must log into a single server in New York to process any data. Maximum control, single point of failure.
2
DuplicatedDevelopment occurs at home base, but operations are handed to autonomous units in foreign locations. Example: HQ builds billing software and sends a copy to Brazil to run independently. Same software, local operation.
3
DecentralizedEach foreign unit designs its own solutions entirely. Example: the German branch uses one payroll system, Singapore uses a completely different one that fits their local laws better. Maximum local fit, minimum standardisation.
4
NetworkedDevelopment and operations occur in a coordinated fashion across ALL units. Example: a global team of developers from various countries working together on a single cloud platform used by the entire firm. Best for transnational strategies.

Global IS Challenges

Agreeing on Common Requirements

Challenge: an ERP rollout fails because local managers feel it was forced upon them by HQ without understanding their workflow. Solution: allow local input into design — invite country CEOs to vote on layouts and processes.

Changing Business Processes

Challenge: US has Sales Tax, Nepal has VAT — the same system must handle both. Solution: create a short list of core business processes with a global customer ID, but allow change in the tax field based on country.

Coordinating App Development

Challenge: launching a massive all-in-one global platform at once causes crashes and data loss. Solution: incremental steps — reduce transnational systems to a minimum and expand carefully.

Coordinating Software Releases

Challenge: Nepal is on Version 2.0, US is on Version 1.5 — files are incompatible. Solution: enforce simultaneous global updates — all operating units upgrade at the same time.

Cooptation

Strategy for managing resistance: bring the opposition into the design and implementation process without giving up control over direction. Example: the manager who hates the new HR system becomes the "Lead Consultant for European Rollout" — he feels empowered, but central team keeps control.

Global IT Challenges

Area Challenge Solution
Computing Platforms Determining how new core systems fit with existing applications developed globally by different divisions. Establishing uniform standards for data, interfaces, and software. Standardisation policies, phased integration roadmap

Business Intelligence (BI)

Business Intelligence refers to software that analyses large datasets to extract insights that support better decision-making. Organisations leverage it to identify optimal global systems for revenue generation.

① Reporting

Processing data via filtering and sorting to create formatted summaries. Example: a monthly PDF report sent to the CFO showing total sales in Europe vs. Asia, sorted by product category. Provides insights to inform decisions.

② Data Mining

Software that searches through data using complex statistical calculations — finding trends, patterns, correlations. Example: an algorithm discovers customers who buy coffee are 40% more likely to buy a specific brand of biscuits → new bundle deal created.

③ Knowledge Management

Creating value from intellectual property by sharing knowledge across the organisation. Example: an internal Wiki where engineers in Singapore share how they solved a machinery breakdown so engineers in Mexico can fix it faster next time.

④ Expert Systems

Intelligent systems with a Knowledge Base and Inference Engine to assist in decision-making. Example: a medical diagnostic tool that asks a doctor about symptoms and uses a database of medical rules to suggest the most likely illness.

ESG in Global Systems

Environmental

Green Computing: energy-efficient cooling in data centres. Server Consolidation: consolidating 50 physical servers into 5 virtual ones to reduce electricity consumption. Energy Efficient Hardware: cloud infrastructure reduces carbon emissions vs. on-premise.

Social

Accessibility: ensuring technology is usable by everyone regardless of physical ability or geographic location. Example: developing a "Lite" version of software that runs on low bandwidth for students in remote areas with poor internet.

Governance

Ethical Data Governance: transparent data collection and compliance with protection laws. Cybersecurity Governance: mandatory MFA for all global employees to protect sensitive intellectual property.

The Bullwhip Effect

SC Coordination Failure · Causes · Cascading Impact · Solutions

What is the Bullwhip Effect?

The Bullwhip Effect (also called the Whiplash Effect) is the phenomenon where small, relatively stable changes in customer demand at the retail end of a supply chain get progressively amplified as you move upstream — from retailer → wholesaler → distributor → manufacturer — resulting in massive, wasteful production swings at the factory despite calm end-consumer demand.

The Analogy: Think of cracking a bullwhip. A tiny flick of your wrist (the handle = end-consumer demand) creates a dramatic, violent snap at the tip (the factory). That tiny wrist movement = 5% more pasta bought by consumers. The violent snap = manufacturer ramping up production 40%.

Step-by-Step: How It Happens

1
Consumer Level — Small SignalCustomers buy 5% more pasta than usual this week. Actual demand change: small and temporary.
2
Retailer Level — First AmplificationThe retailer sees the 5% increase and, fearing a stockout, orders 10% more from the wholesaler "just to be safe." The order signal has already doubled in size.
3
Wholesaler Level — Second AmplificationThe wholesaler sees the retailer's 10% increase and orders 20% more from the manufacturer — because they're also worried about future spikes and want their own safety buffer.
4
Manufacturer Level — Full AmplificationThe manufacturer sees this massive spike — a 20% increase in orders — and ramps up production by 40%, hiring extra staff and buying enormous quantities of raw materials. A 5% consumer change caused a 40% factory swing.

Root Causes

Order Batching

Instead of ordering little and often, companies batch orders weekly or monthly. This creates artificial demand spikes that don't reflect real underlying consumer purchasing patterns.

Demand Forecasting Errors

Each link in the chain adds its own safety buffer on top of the last layer's already-inflated estimate. Errors compound exponentially as you move upstream.

Price Fluctuations

Promotions and discounts encourage "forward buying" — customers buy in bulk during sales, artificially inflating demand signals that cause upstream overproduction.

Shortage Gaming

When products are scarce, buyers over-order to guarantee supply. Then when the shortage ends, they cancel orders — creating a sudden crash in apparent demand that is just as disruptive as the spike.

Impact Table — What Lack of Coordination Does

Performance Measure Impact of Lack of Coordination
Manufacturing cost 📈 Increases — overtime, emergency production runs
Inventory cost 📈 Increases — safety stock, warehousing costs
Replenishment lead time 📈 Increases — suppliers overwhelmed by spikes
Transportation cost 📈 Increases — emergency air freight, rush shipments
Shipping and receiving cost 📈 Increases — irregular, unpredictable volumes
Level of product availability 📉 Decreases — stockouts despite overproduction elsewhere
Profitability 📉 Decreases — entire chain loses value simultaneously

The Only Real Source of Cash

The end customer is the only source of real cash entering the entire supply chain. Every payment between partners is simply redistribution of that same pool of money. No new cash is created by moving goods between partners. This is why the Bullwhip Effect is so damaging — it creates costs without creating any new customer value.

How to Control the Bullwhip Effect

1
Demand Visibility — Point-of-Sale Data SharingShare real POS data with all upstream partners so everyone reacts to actual consumer demand, not distorted orders. Example: Kimberly-Clark and Walmart share live sales data — the manufacturer knows exactly how many diapers sold yesterday and adjusts production accordingly.
2
Vendor-Managed Inventory (VMI)Let the supplier manage replenishment directly based on real inventory data — cutting out order distortion entirely. The supplier monitors your warehouse levels and ships automatically when thresholds are reached.
3
CPFR — Collaborative Planning, Forecasting & ReplenishmentJoint forecasting across all supply chain partners to align production with true demand. Partners share past sales, current inventory, and upcoming promotions — then build one shared forecast everyone works from.
4
Smaller, More Frequent OrdersReduce order batching to prevent demand spikes from forming. Instead of one large monthly order, place smaller weekly orders. This reflects real consumption patterns much more accurately.
5
Stable Pricing PoliciesEliminate promotions that cause "forward buying" and artificial demand surges. Everyday low pricing (EDLP) — as used by Walmart — removes the incentive to stockpile during sales.

Supply Chain Management II

Why Supply Chain Strategy Matters

All functional areas — product development, marketing, and supply chain — must work together as one coordinated strategy. A company that promises fast delivery but runs a slow, cost-efficiency-focused supply chain will fail because its processes don't match its promises to customers.

Competitive Strategy

Defines which customer needs to satisfy — cheapest, fastest, highest quality, most convenient. This is the north star that all other strategies must align to.

Product Development Strategy

What products to create to fulfil the competitive promise. Must consider supply chain feasibility — can we actually make and deliver what we're promising?

Marketing & Sales Strategy

How to segment markets, price, and promote products. Must be aligned with what the supply chain can actually deliver consistently.

Supply Chain Strategy

The operational "how-to": procurement, transportation, manufacturing, distribution. Must match the competitive strategy exactly — this is where strategic fit either exists or doesn't.

Strategic Fit

Strategic Fit = when Competitive Strategy and Supply Chain Strategy have aligned goals. Amazon's competitive strategy is fast, affordable delivery — its supply chain strategy (massive warehouses, own logistics, same-day delivery) is perfectly aligned. That's strategic fit. A company fails when its processes do not match its promises.

Supply Chain Integration — Two Pillars

Information Sharing

Partners (suppliers, manufacturers, retailers) share real-time data — inventory levels, sales data, production schedules. Benefits: reduces bullwhip effect, enables early problem detection, builds trust, allows quick response to market changes.

Coordinated Workflow

Partners align production, procurement, and operations schedules. Benefits: production efficiencies, no costly delays, fewer defects, faster time to market. Both pillars together = a truly integrated supply chain.

CPFR — Collaborative Planning, Forecasting & Replenishment

CPFR is the process where two or more supply chain companies synchronise their demand forecasts into a single shared plan. All partners work from the same data — eliminating the information distortion that causes the Bullwhip Effect.

Past Sales Trends

Historical data to identify seasonal patterns, growth trends, and demand cycles shared across all partners.

Point-of-Sale Data

Real-time consumer purchase data from the cash register — the most accurate signal of actual demand in the system.

On-Hand Inventory

Current stock levels at all partners in the chain — prevents ordering when stock already exists.

Scheduled Promotions

Upcoming sales events that will spike demand — warning all partners in advance so they can prepare without panic ordering.

Transportation — Modes Compared

Transportation is the physical link between supply chain stages. Faster modes = more responsive but less efficient (higher cost). The right mode depends on the product's value, perishability, and urgency.

Mode Best For Pros Cons
Rail Bulk goods, raw materials, high-density cargo Low cost for large volumes; reliable scheduling Slow, inflexible, not economical for small loads, limited routes
Trucking Small loads, point-to-point delivery, last-mile Flexible routes, reliable, less damage to goods More expensive than rail for long distances, traffic delays
Air High-value, perishable, urgent goods Fastest mode, least theft, global reach Most expensive by far, weight limits
Water / Sea International bulk shipping, containers Lowest cost per unit, massive capacity Slowest mode, port dependency, weather risk
Pipeline Oil, gas, liquids Low operating cost, reliable, long operational life High capital cost, limited to liquid/gas, fixed routes
Intermodal Long-distance + last-mile combination Combines efficiency of sea/rail with truck flexibility Complex logistics coordination, container handling costs

Sourcing: In-House vs Outsource

In-House (Make)

Choose when: outsourcing risk is significant, OR when the activity is a core competency that defines your competitive advantage and you don't want competitors to access it. Also when quality control is critical and cannot be trusted to a third party.

Outsource (Buy)

Choose when: you need to increase efficiency, OR when you need specialised expertise that isn't your core skill. Outsourcing non-core activities frees resources to focus on what you do best — and reduces fixed costs.

A global supply chain is the international network used to source raw materials, manufacture products, and deliver to customers across countries.

System Security

CIA Triad · Threats · Malware · Online Crime · Security Tools

Origin of Computer Security

Computer security was originally engineered by the US Department of Defense to protect military intelligence. As machines evolved from isolated machines to interconnected networks, with more users accessing systems, the vulnerability and risk increased dramatically. Today computers are the backbone of the economy — processing financial payments, purchase orders, and legal authorisations — making security a global concern, not just a technical one.

Key Security Terminology

Term Definition Example
Computer Security Protection of computer assets from unauthorised access, use, alteration, or destruction. Firewalls, encrypted databases, off-site backups
Physical Security Tangible protection methods for hardware and premises. Alarms, security guards, fences, bombproof server rooms
Logical Security Protection of digital (non-physical) assets through software and procedures. Multi-factor authentication, complex passwords, access controls
System Vulnerability A state that allows attackers to execute commands or access restricted data. Outdated OS, unpatched software, default passwords left unchanged
Computer Security Risk Anything that could lead to data theft, damage, or system disruption. Phishing links, weak passwords, unencrypted wi-fi
Countermeasure A procedure to recognise, reduce, and eliminate threats before or after they occur. Installing antivirus, training staff, enabling MFA

The CIA Triad — Three Pillars of Security

🔒 Confidentiality

Data is accessible ONLY to authorised individuals. Includes Privacy (the user controls what information is collected about them) and Secrecy (hiding sensitive data from unauthorised parties using encryption and access controls).

✔ Integrity

Data received is exactly what was sent — not altered by unauthorised parties during transmission or storage. Example: preventing an attacker from changing a $100 payment authorisation to $1,000 in transit.

⚡ Necessity (Availability)

Authorised users have reliable access to systems and data when they need it. Example: preventing denial-of-service attacks that block legitimate users from accessing their banking app or online store.

Confidentiality Threats

Threat How It Works Solution
Sniffer Program Software (e.g., Wireshark) that captures network packets on unencrypted channels like public Wi-Fi — reads your data as it travels. Encryption, VPN, detect unauthorised programs on network
Backdoor Electronic holes that allow access without authentication — often installed by malware like Netcat. The attacker enters without any login. Firewalls, access controls, isolate systems for confidential data
Corporate Info Theft Stealing trade secrets, customer databases via malware, phishing, or physical theft by insiders or outsiders. Security training, physical protections (server room access), DLP software
IP Address Exposure Your IP address, browser type, and version are automatically revealed when browsing — can be used to identify and target you. VPN (paid, strong encryption) masks your true IP address
E-Commerce Threat Users unknowingly share location, credentials, and app permissions on fake or poorly secured sites. MFA, check privacy settings and permissions, HTTPS verification

Integrity Threats

Active vs Passive Wiretapping

Active: altering message streams — changing $100 to $1,000 during transmission. Passive: listening only, without altering data — still a confidentiality breach but not an integrity attack.

Cyber Vandalism

Damaging or destroying digital property — hacking a website and replacing its content with the attacker's own content, images, or messages. Causes reputational damage.

Masquerading / Spoofing

Attacker pretends to be someone else. Includes DNS spoofing: substituting a fake website address for the real one — users think they're on their bank's site but they're on the attacker's copy.

Phishing

Tricking victims into revealing confidential information through fake emails or texts. Creates urgency ("Your account will be closed!"), asks for passwords, or installs malware via attachments.

Necessity / Availability Threats

DoS — Denial of Service

Attack from one computer sending millions of fake requests so real users can't access the service. The server is overwhelmed handling fake traffic. Example: fake login requests blocking real customers from accessing banking.

DDoS — Distributed Denial of Service

Same concept but uses thousands of hijacked devices (a botnet) simultaneously from around the world. Dramatically harder to block because traffic comes from millions of legitimate-looking IP addresses.

Malware Types — Detailed

Type How It Spreads Primary Goal Distinguishing Feature
Virus Attaches itself to a legitimate file — needs human action (opening an email attachment) to activate and spread. Corrupt or delete data Requires human trigger to activate
Worm Spreads automatically across networks without any human help — copies itself repeatedly from machine to machine. Consume bandwidth, crash systems Self-propagating — no human action needed
Trojan Horse Disguises itself as something useful (free game, useful software) and gets installed willingly by the user — then activates its malicious payload. Create a backdoor for hackers to enter Appears legitimate — social engineering
Spyware Usually bundled silently with other software downloads — installs itself and begins collecting user information without their knowledge. Steal passwords, track browsing, harvest credentials Silent, invisible — user unaware it's running

Online Crime Terminology

Term Meaning Example
Computer Crime Illegal act where a computer is used as a tool to commit the crime. Printing counterfeit money using a computer and printer
Cyber Crime Criminal activities carried out via the internet. Computer + Network + Crime. Online scams, identity theft, online fraud
Cyber War A country's critical information systems paralysed by massive destructive software attacks from a hostile state. Attacks on power grids, financial systems, military networks
Cyber Terrorism Politically motivated internet attacks designed to cause disruption, fear, or damage in society — targeting infrastructure. Attacking a country's water treatment control systems

Managing Systems Security

IT Risk Management · Security Policy · Cryptography · SSL · Forensics

IT Risk Management

IT Risk Management is the process of identifying, assessing, mitigating, and monitoring information technology systems, networks, and data security risks. The core formula is:

Risk = Probability × Impact (Cost)

A risk with low probability but catastrophic impact (like a server fire) and a risk with high probability but low impact (like a minor system hiccup) require very different management strategies.

The IT Risk Management Model — Four Quadrants

Quadrant I — High Probability, Low Impact
Contain & Control
Happens frequently but isn't devastating. Manage through standard operating procedures and monitoring to keep frequency and cost under control. Example: minor system slow-downs during peak usage.
Quadrant II — High Probability, High Impact
Prevent at All Costs
Most critical category — both likely AND catastrophic. Must take active, expensive measures to stop these from occurring at all. Example: frequent DDoS attacks on a financial services site.
Quadrant III — Low Probability, Low Impact
Ignore (Accept)
Unlikely and low damage. The cost of implementing complex security often outweighs any benefit. Accepted as a cost of doing business. Example: minor website defacement in a low-profile niche industry.
Quadrant IV — Low Probability, High Impact
Insurance or Disaster Plan
Catastrophic if it happens, but unlikely. Transfer risk via insurance or ensure fast recovery through disaster recovery and business continuity plans. Example: a regional earthquake destroying the data centre.
Real-World Example — Tornadoes in Japan

Yokohama (frequent tornadoes): Computer systems must be actively protected → Quadrant II — Prevent

Tottori (rare tornadoes): Insurance and a backup plan suffices → Quadrant IV — Insurance

Security Policy

A Security Policy is a formal, enforceable document that outlines how an organisation will protect its information assets. Its main goal is to protect three things: Confidentiality | Integrity | Availability (Necessity).

Elements of a Security Policy

Element What It Covers
Assets Identification A clear inventory of all hardware, software, and data assets, plus justification for protection based on the Risk Model. You can't protect what you haven't catalogued.
Protection Responsibility Who is responsible for the security of specific systems — defining ownership, accountability, and chain of command for security incidents.
Behaviour Standards What is acceptable (strong passwords, locked screens) and what is unacceptable (sharing credentials, installing unauthorised software). Written rules employees must follow.
Technical & Operational Controls Access authorisations, virus protection tools, physical security (locked server rooms), and disaster recovery plans. The implementation of the policy in actual systems.

Information Classification

Public Information

Information that can be shared externally without harm to the organisation. Examples: press releases, product brochures, annual reports. Requires minimal protection — it's meant to be seen.

Company Confidential

Sensitive information like strategic plans, financial projections, internal processes, and employee data that could harm the company if leaked. Requires strict access controls and encryption.

Steps to Create a Security Policy

1
Determine Assets and ThreatsKnow what is valuable — data, hardware, software, intellectual property, brand reputation — and identify what could threaten each asset. This forms the foundation of the entire policy.
2
Determine Access NeedsDefine who — employees, contractors, partners — needs access to what systems to do their jobs. Apply the principle of least privilege: give only the access needed, nothing more.
3
Identify Protection ResourcesDecide on the tools needed: firewalls, antivirus software, encryption, physical access controls, staff training programmes. Match resources to risk level from the quadrant model.
4
Develop Written Security PolicyFormalise all rules, procedures, and expectations into an official document. Must be clear, specific, and enforceable — vague policies are useless policies.
5
Commit ResourcesProvide the budget, time, and leadership support to actually implement and enforce it. A security policy that exists only on paper and is never enforced provides zero protection.

Digital Certificate, Cryptography & PKI

Cryptography

The process of hiding or encoding information using mathematical techniques so only the intended recipient — with the correct key — can read it. The message is scrambled into ciphertext for transmission and unscrambled at the destination.

PKI — Public Key Infrastructure

A system for creating, storing, and distributing digital certificates. Contains the policies, hardware, software, and procedures needed to manage public-key encryption. The backbone of internet security.

Certification Authority (CA)

The organisation that issues digital certificates and vouches for their authenticity. Analogy: CA = Passport Office; Digital Certificate = Your Passport. Without a trusted CA, you cannot verify who you're actually talking to online.

Digital Certificate

An electronic "passport" that proves the authenticity of a user, device, or server. Contains the owner's public key, identity information, and the CA's digital signature. Used in HTTPS, email signing, and software verification.

Steganography

Steganography is the art of hiding information within other, non-secret data. Unlike cryptography (which scrambles a message so it can't be read), steganography hides the very existence of the message. The secret message is invisible to anyone who doesn't know to look for it. Example: embedding a copyright watermark invisibly within a digital photograph — only special software reveals it.

Hash Coding

A hash code is a fixed-size string generated from data using a hash function — acting as a unique digital fingerprint to ensure data integrity. Hash functions are one-way: you can generate the hash from data, but you cannot reverse-engineer the data from the hash.

Key Properties of Hash Functions

• One-way process — cannot be reversed like encryption
• Even a single character change produces a completely different hash
• Websites store hashed passwords, not actual passwords
• Used to verify file integrity after download or transmission

Example — SHA-256 in Action

"HELLO" → generates hash 52
"HELLP" → generates hash 53
One letter changed = completely different fingerprint. If someone tampers with data in transit, the hash will not match and the tampering is detected immediately.

SSL — Secure Sockets Layer Protocol

SSL creates an encrypted link between a web server and a browser, ensuring data privacy, authentication, and integrity during transmission. The core concept is the "Handshake."

Analogy: Without SSL, sending data over the internet is like writing on a postcard — anyone handling it can read it. SSL turns that postcard into a sealed, tamper-proof, encrypted envelope that only the recipient can open.
1
Client HelloBrowser announces to the server what encryption algorithms it supports and what key lengths it can handle.
2
Server Responds + Sends CertificateServer replies and sends its Digital Certificate (containing its public key) to prove its identity. Client verifies the certificate was signed by a trusted CA.
3
Client Creates and Sends Session KeyClient generates a Session Key (a one-time symmetric key for this session only), encrypts it with the server's public key, and sends it. Only the server's private key can decrypt this.
4
Secure Session BeginsBoth sides now communicate using the shared session key. All data is encrypted — anyone intercepting the traffic sees only meaningless gibberish. Session key is discarded when the session ends.
Term Meaning
HTTP HyperText Transfer Protocol — sends data in plain text. Completely insecure for sensitive information.
HTTPS HTTP Secure — uses SSL/TLS certificate. All data is encrypted in both directions.
Session Key Temporary, one-time-use symmetric key for encrypting a single web session. Discarded when you close the connection.
Protocol A set of rules for formatting and processing data so that different computers can understand each other.

Other Security Tools

Access Control

Defines who can access the computer and what actions they are authorised to take. Based on identity, role, and need-to-know. Prevents unauthorised users from reaching sensitive systems.

Audit Trail

Records all access attempts: who logged in, at what time, from which IP address, and which files they accessed. Essential for forensic investigation and compliance reporting.

Intrusion Detection

Analyses network traffic in real time, assesses vulnerabilities, and identifies suspicious behaviour patterns. Triggers alerts when something unusual is detected — like a login at 3am from a foreign country.

Firewall

A security barrier (hardware, software, or both) that controls which network packets are allowed in or out. All traffic must pass through it; only authorised traffic passes; designed to be immune to penetration itself.

Computer Forensics & Ethical Hacking

Computer Forensics

Using investigative techniques to identify, collect, preserve, and analyse evidence from computer devices — often for use in court. Must follow strict chain-of-custody procedures to ensure evidence is admissible. Responsible for collection, preservation, and analysis of computer-related evidence after a security incident.

Ethical Hacking (White Hat)

Testing computer systems, networks, or applications to find security vulnerabilities — using the same tools and techniques as malicious hackers but with the legal owner's explicit permission. Companies hire ethical hackers ("White Hats") to find weaknesses before the "Black Hats" do.

e-Business vs e-Commerce — The Critical Distinction

e-Business (The Broader Term)

Conducting all business activities using telecommunications networks — especially the Internet. This includes servicing customers online, collaborating with partners and suppliers, managing internal operations digitally, and R&D. e-Business is much more than just buying and selling — it's the digitalisation of the entire enterprise.

e-Commerce (The Subset)

Conducting commerce specifically — buying and selling — electronically using the Internet. e-Commerce is one specific aspect of e-Business. All e-Commerce is e-Business, but not all e-Business is e-Commerce.

Business Models

Dot-Com (Pure Online)

Business operating ONLY online with no physical stores. Lower overhead costs but dependent entirely on digital infrastructure. Example: early Amazon (books only), eBay, Booking.com.

Bricks and Clicks (Hybrid)

Business operating BOTH online AND via physical stores — the best of both worlds. Example: modern Amazon (online + Amazon Go stores), Walmart (stores + Walmart.com), most banks.

Bricks and Mortar (Pure Physical)

Business operating ONLY via physical stores with no meaningful online presence. Increasingly rare and vulnerable to digital disruption. Example: small local shops without websites.

Categories of e-Commerce

Category Description Example
B2C — Business to Consumer Businesses selling directly to individual consumers via the web. The most visible form of e-commerce to everyday people. Amazon, Daraz, Flipkart
B2B — Business to Business Transactions between businesses — procurement departments negotiating with suppliers, bulk purchasing, digital invoicing. Much larger in total volume than B2C. Alibaba wholesale, SAP procurement systems
Business Processes Companies using internet technologies to support their own internal buying/selling operations — ERP systems, logistics management, digital supply chain platforms. SAP ERP, Oracle Supply Chain
C2C — Consumer to Consumer Individuals buying and selling among themselves using a platform as intermediary. The platform earns fees without owning inventory. eBay auctions, Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist
B2G — Business to Government Business transactions with government agencies — digital procurement, tax filing, license applications, reporting requirements. Paying taxes online, submitting compliance reports digitally

What Accelerated e-Business Growth — Four Drivers

Wide Public Network Access

The Internet — a network anyone can connect to — created the infrastructure needed for global commerce. Without universal access, e-business is impossible.

Internet Technology (TCP/IP)

Standardised protocols reduced network implementation and training costs dramatically. Companies no longer needed proprietary networks — one shared global standard.

Standard User Interface

Web browsers (Netscape, then Internet Explorer, then Chrome) gave everyone a common, easy-to-use way to access the internet — no technical expertise required.

e-Business Applications

Apps running on the internet drastically reduced the cost of communication, transaction processing, and customer acquisition compared to traditional methods.

Benefits of e-Business

Global Accessibility

The internet removes geographical constraints — businesses reach customers worldwide without opening physical offices in every country.

24/7 Availability

Online stores never close — a customer in Tokyo can buy from a Kathmandu business at 3am. Maximises every possible revenue opportunity.

Direct Marketing

Manufacturers can bypass retailers and distributors entirely — selling directly to end customers, capturing the full margin and building direct relationships.

Closer Customer Relationships

Quick response to concerns, personalised recommendations, loyalty programs, and direct communication increase customer retention.

Reduced Order Processing

Automated processing improves efficiency, reduces errors, and eliminates the cost of manual order entry — orders flow directly into fulfilment systems.

Customer Data & Insights

Direct digital customer link provides instant feedback on product demand, browsing behaviour, and preferences — enabling data-driven decisions.

e-Commerce Barriers

Fraud

Security mechanisms for digital payments remain a major concern. Credit card fraud, identity theft, and fake storefronts undermine consumer trust and require expensive security infrastructure.

Product Limitations

Perishable foods, high-cost unique items (antiques, fine art), and products requiring physical inspection or fitting (prescription glasses, custom tailoring) are poor fits for pure e-commerce.

Technology Access

Requires a critical mass of customers with appropriate technology access — internet connection, smart device, digital literacy, and payment methods. A significant barrier in developing markets.

Regulations

Cultural and legal barriers vary dramatically across countries — different privacy laws, payment regulations, import restrictions, and consumer protection requirements complicate global e-commerce.

Secure e-Commerce — Five Requirements

2
Layered SecurityMultiple security layers create multiple obstacles that attackers must break through — WAF (Web Application Firewall), CDN security, application-level security, and database security all working together.
