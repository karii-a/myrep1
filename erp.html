Lecture 1 // Chapter 01

Introduction to Enterprise Systems & SAP

This chapter establishes what information systems and ERP are, how SAP is structured, and how data is categorized within an enterprise system. These concepts underpin every subsequent chapter.

What is an Information System?

An Information System (IS) is a set of interrelated components that collect, retrieve, process, store, and distribute information for planning, control, coordination, analysis, and decision-making.

IS types: General systems (broad-purpose) vs. Specialized systems (built for specific functions like accounting or HR).

Centralized: All data in one place — easier to control but single point of failure.
Decentralized: Multiple servers share data — more resilient but harder to synchronize.

What is ERP?

Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) is a large, integrated software package that manages a firm's business processes across the entire enterprise.

  • Integrates operations, processes, and information flows
  • Standard software configured to specific company needs
  • Database-driven: Input, Storage, Manipulation, Output
  • Promotes better decision-making through shared data
  • Reduces IT maintenance vs. siloed legacy systems

ERP Types: On-Premise, Cloud-Based, Hybrid

About SAP

SAP stands for Systeme, Anwendungen und Produkte in der Datenverarbeitung — "Systems, Applications, and Products in Data Processing."

Founded in Walldorf, Germany in 1972. World's largest business software company and third-largest independent software provider.

  • 45,000+ employees in 50+ countries
  • 95,000+ customers in 120+ countries
  • 12 million users worldwide
Functional vs. Cross-Functional Structure

Functional Structure: Departments (Sales, Accounting, Purchasing, Production) operate independently → silos cause delays, competing interests, project overruns.

Cross-Functional Structure: Breaks down silos; different departments collaborate around shared processes. ERP is the technological backbone enabling this.

A business process is a collection of coherent activities that starts with a trigger and ends with delivery of products or services.

Three Types of Data in ERP
  • Organizational Data: Represents enterprise structure — Client, Company Code, Plant, Storage Location
  • Master Data: Key business entities created once, reused many times — Customer Master, Vendor Master, Material Master
  • Transactional Data: Reflects execution of process steps — dates, quantities, amounts recorded during sales orders or goods receipts

The Global Bike Inc. (GBI) Case Study

GBI is the fictional but realistic company used as the SAP training environment. Understanding its history, organizational structure, products, and business processes is essential for all subsequent labs and concepts.

GBI History & Background
  • 1990: John Davis (US mountain bike champion) founded Frankenstein Bikes
  • 1993: Peter Schwarz (German composite engineer) founded Heidelberg Composites
  • 2000: John and Peter met
  • 2001: Merger created Global Bike Inc.

GBI operates as a global concern with entities in the US and Germany, using the full capabilities of SAP ERP.

Leadership & Strategy

John Davis (Co-CEO): Sales, Marketing, Service & Support, IT, Finance, HR — "sells products and brings in money."

Peter Schwarz (Co-CEO): Research, Design, Procurement, Manufacturing — "builds products and spends money."

Strategic Focus: World-class bicycle company for professional athletes and prosumers — emphasizing Quality, Toughness, and Performance.

Cross-Functional Integration

Order-to-Cash (OTC): Customer order → payment. Integrates SD, MM, and FI. A single sales order triggers activities in all three modules automatically.

Procure-to-Pay (P2P): Need identified → vendor paid. Integrates MM and FI. A purchase order triggers goods receipt, inventory updates, and automatic journal entries.

This real-time integration eliminates manual re-keying and ensures data consistency across the entire enterprise.

MM Master Data

Vendor Master: All info for business activities with an external supplier. Used by Purchasing and Accounting. Created as Business Partner with roles FLVN00 (FI) and FLVN01 (Purchasing). Contains General Data, Company Code Data, and Purchasing Org Data.

Material Master: All information to manage a material throughout its lifecycle. Used by SD, MM, PP, FI/CO, QM. Organized into functional Views: Basic Data, Sales, Purchasing, MRP, Storage, Accounting, Quality.

Purchasing Info Record: Links a specific vendor to a specific material. Stores pricing, last PO number, delivery tolerances, and lead times.

Purchase Order Structure

A PO is a formal, legally binding request to a vendor. Three structural levels:

LevelContains
HeaderVendor, doc number, date, currency, payment terms, total value
Item OverviewMaterials, quantities, delivery dates, price per UoM

Goods Receipt & Invoice Verification

Goods Receipt (GR): Physical acceptance of goods into SAP. Creates Material Document, Accounting Document. Updates stock quantities/values and PO history.

Key movement types: 101 (GR into warehouse), 103 (GR blocked stock), 122 (return to vendor), 561 (initial stock entry).

Three-Way Match: Invoice verified against PO (target) and GR (actual). Discrepancies generate warnings or block payment.

Warehouse Management (WM)

WM manages the physical storage and movement of materials within a warehouse complex at bin level — far more granular than Inventory Management (MM), which only tracks at the storage location level.

Key WM Documents

Transfer Requirement (TR): A request to transfer materials from source to destination bin at a specific time. Represents expected/scheduled movements. Auto-generated by IM postings.

Transfer Order (TO): The central execution document in WM. Every material movement — real or logical — requires a TO. Contains source bin, destination bin, material, quantity.

Posting Change Notice (PCN): Generated by posting change processes for WM-administered stocks — similar to TR but not always combined with physical movement.

Types of Goods Movements

Transfer Posting: Changes stock type, material number, or batch. May involve physical movement. Types: material-to-material, stock type-to-stock type, consignment to own stock, plant-to-plant.

Stock Transfer: Physical material movement. Done in one step (simultaneous remove + place) or two steps (separate remove from storage and place in storage).

Stock Transport Order (STO): Purchase order used to transfer material from one plant to another within the same enterprise.

Delivery Scheduling

Backward Scheduling: Starts from customer's requested delivery date, works backward to calculate material availability, loading, picking, and transportation dates → gives required order date.

Forward Scheduling: Used when requested date cannot be met. Starts from earliest material availability, works forward to find earliest possible delivery.

GBI Standard Parameters:
Transit Time: 2 days | Loading: 1 day
Transport Scheduling: 1 day | Pick & Pack: 2 days

Sales Order Automatic Determinations

When a sales order is created, SAP automatically determines:

  • Delivery Scheduling — confirmed delivery date via backward/forward scheduling
  • Shipping Point — which plant location processes the shipment
  • Route — transportation path from GBI warehouse to customer
  • ATP Check — confirms stock availability on the required date
  • Transfer to MRP — insufficient stock triggers planning
  • Pricing — price, discounts, surcharges, taxes, freight
  • Credit Limit Check — verifies customer's credit exposure
Post Goods Issue & Billing

Post Goods Issue (PGI): The critical event in the shipping process that:

  • Reduces inventory in the system
  • Records Cost of Goods Sold
  • Updates the General Ledger
  • Signals legal transfer of ownership to the customer

Billing: Creates Accounts Receivable debit to customer sub-ledger and credit to revenue account. Can be order-based, delivery-based, collective, or split invoicing.

Document Flow: Inquiry → Quotation → Sales Order → Delivery → Billing → Accounting Document. Provides full audit trail.

Bill of Materials (BOM)

A BOM is the structured list of all components needed to produce a product — like a recipe's ingredients list.

Example GBI Bike BOM includes: Wheel Assembly, Frame, Handle Bar, Seat Kit, Pedal Assembly, Chain, Derailleur Gear Assembly, Brake Kit, Warranty Document, Packaging.

BOM Types:
Single-Level: Only direct components
Multi-Level: Explodes all levels (finished → sub-assembly → raw material)
Variant BOM: Multiple product variants sharing common parts

MRP — 5 Logical Steps
StepAction
1. Net RequirementsAvailable stock + scheduled receipts − gross requirements = shortage
2. Lot SizeDetermines how much to order/produce (Static, Periodic, Optimized)
3. Procurement TypeBuy externally (→ Purchase Requisition) or produce internally (→ Planned Order)
4. SchedulingCalculates start/finish dates working backward from requirement date
5. BOM ExplosionGenerates dependent requirements for all sub-assemblies and raw materials
Routing & Work Centers

If BOM = ingredients list, Routing = cooking instructions. Defines what operation to perform, at which Work Center, for how long, in what sequence.

Work Center: Where and when an operation is performed — can be a machine, person, production line, or group. Defines available capacity and charge rates.

Example: Operation 20 = "Attach seat to frame" → Work Center ASSY1000 → Time = 1 minute.

Planning Strategies:
Make-to-Stock (MTS): Build ahead of orders using forecasts
Make-to-Order (MTO): Produce only when customer orders

Financial Accounting (FI)

FI collects all transactional data and provides the foundation for statutory financial reports. It integrates seamlessly with MM, SD, and PP to eliminate manual re-keying while providing a complete audit trail.

The Three Core Financial Statements

Balance Sheet: Snapshot of financial position at a point in time. Assets = Liabilities + Equity

  • Assets: cash, receivables, inventory, equipment
  • Liabilities: accounts payable, accruals, long-term debt
  • Equity: share capital + retained earnings

Income Statement (P&L): Performance over a period. Net Income = Revenues − Expenses

Statement of Cash Flows: Cash changes in Operating, Investing, and Financing activities.

SAP Document Principle

A core architectural concept ensuring internal controls:

  • Every business transaction creates a uniquely numbered electronic document stored permanently
  • Documents cannot be deleted — only reversed (original remains)
  • Each document records: responsible person, date/time, commercial content
  • Full Audit Trails — trace any balance back to individual transactions
  • Required by law in most countries worldwide

Key Concept — G/L & Sub-Ledger Relationship
The General Ledger contains the total balance for each account (e.g., Accounts Receivable = $850). Individual customer sub-ledgers break this down (Customer 142: $100, Customer 189: $400, Customer 135: $150, Customer 123: $200). Postings to sub-ledgers automatically update the G/L reconciliation account — both are always in sync.
